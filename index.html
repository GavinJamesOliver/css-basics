What is a Treaty:

A treaty is a formal agreement between nations. The treaty establishes the responsibilities of each nation for dealing with each other and is legally binding. In the case of the numbered treaties in Canada between the Canadian government and the Indigenous peoples. The numbered treaties of Canada provided the framework for how the Indigenous and t he Canadian government would live together and share the land.

There was a huge difference between how the Indigenous saw the treaties and how the government did. For the Indigenous a treaty meant to bring things together and sacred promises. They saw the treaty as a way to build relationships and share the land. This contrasts with the Canadian government who used words like surrendered or gave up. To them the treaties were a way to make the Indigenous give up their land.

Treaty 2:

Treaty 2 was signed shortly after Treaty 1. Signed in August 1871 in Manitoba, the land of Treaty 2 extends North and west of the Treaty 1 territory and is three times the size of Manitoba at the time. The treaty contains 21,440,909 acres of land or roughly 86,768 square kilometers.

The Canadian Government aimed to take the Indigenous peoples land and assimilate them and their culture into European society. Indigenous people were concerned for their lands and traditional ways and wanted to secure their future by forming a good relationship with the Canadian government.
